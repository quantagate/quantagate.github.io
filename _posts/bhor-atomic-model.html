The Bohr Atomic Model

A quantum leap in understanding the atom.

A Revolutionary Idea in 1913

Proposed by Danish physicist Niels Bohr, the model was a pivotal step that combined classical physics with early quantum theory. It provided the first successful explanation for the discrete emission lines of the hydrogen atom, suggesting that electrons exist in specific, quantized energy orbits around the nucleus, much like planets around a sun.

Electrons occupy discrete, circular orbits (shells K, L, M, etc.) with specific energy levels around a central, positively charged nucleus.

Atomic Composition

The atom consists of a dense nucleus containing protons and neutrons, with much lighter electrons orbiting it. Most of the atom is empty space.

The Four Key Postulates

1

Fixed Orbits

Electrons revolve in stable, circular orbits with definite, quantized energy levels and do not exist in between.

2

Stationary States

While in a fixed orbit, an electron does not radiate energy, defying classical electromagnetic theory.

3

Energy Transitions

Energy is emitted or absorbed only when an electron jumps from one allowed orbit to another.

4

Quantized Momentum

The Quantum Jump: How Light is Born

The model's greatest success was explaining atomic spectra. When an electron absorbs energy, it jumps to a higher orbit (excitation). When it falls back, it releases that energy as a single particle of light (a photon) with a specific wavelength, creating a distinct spectral line.

Explaining the Hydrogen Spectrum

The discrete lines in hydrogen's emission spectrum correspond to electrons falling to specific energy levels. For instance, transitions to the n=2 level produce the visible "Balmer series" of light.

Triumphs and Troubles

Model Applicability

The model is remarkably accurate for hydrogen but fails to predict the complex interactions in multi-electron atoms.

Legacy: A Stepping Stone to Modern Physics

While the Bohr model has been superseded, its core idea of quantized energy levels was revolutionary and laid the essential groundwork for the modern quantum mechanical model, which describes electrons not as particles in fixed orbits, but as probability clouds (orbitals).
